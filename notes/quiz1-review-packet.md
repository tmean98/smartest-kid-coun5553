## 1. KEY DEFINITIONS & TERMS

### The Big Three: Legal vs. Ethical vs. Best Practice
| Term | Definition | Created By |
|------|-----------|------------|
| **Legal standards** | The *minimum* behavior society will tolerate from a professional | Elected officials (legislators); enforced by police; interpreted by judges |
| **Ethical standards** | The *minimum* behavior other counselors will tolerate from counselors | Created by counselors themselves (e.g., ACA Code of Ethics); enforced by ethics committees & licensure boards |
| **Best practice** | The *ideal* for counselor practice — what counselors aspire to | Professional consensus; aspirational, not mandatory |

### Values & Ethics Vocabulary
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Morals** | From Latin *mores* (customs/norms); moral actions determined within broad cultural context or religious standard; personal belief system |
| **Personal values** | Influenced by culture, family, community, beliefs; unique to the individual; guide what is good/right behavior |
| **Professional values** | Identified values of a professional group (e.g., ACA's core values) |
| **Ethics** | A discipline within philosophy concerned with human conduct and moral decision making |
| **Professional ethics** | Conduct judged as good or right for counselors as a professional group |
| **Principle ethics** | Professionally agreed-upon obligations always considered in ethical decision making; asks "What should I do?" |
| **Virtue ethics** | Focus on traits of character/virtue; asks "Who should I be?" (not just as clinician but as a person) |
| **Bracketing** | "The intentional separating of a counselor's personal values from professional values in order to provide ethical and appropriate counseling to all clients" (Kocet & Herlihy, 2014, p. 182) |

### Six Principle Ethics of Counseling (ACA, 2014 Preamble)
| Principle | Definition | Plain English |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|
| **Autonomy** | Fostering the right to control the direction of one's life | Clients choose their own direction; counselors decrease dependency |
| **Nonmaleficence** | Avoiding actions that cause harm | "Do no harm" — the most salient moral principle |
| **Beneficence** | Working for the good of individuals and society | Actively promote mental health and well-being |
| **Justice** | Treating individuals equitably, fostering fairness and equality | Fair treatment, nondiscrimination, equal access |
| **Fidelity** | Honoring commitments and keeping promises; fulfilling responsibilities of trust | Be faithful — especially to confidentiality pledges |
| **Veracity** | Dealing truthfully with individuals | Be honest in all professional contacts |

### Ethical Theories
| Theory | Position |
|--------|----------|
| **Ethical absolutism** | Some absolute moral standards are universally applicable regardless of circumstances |
| **Ethical relativism** | No absolute moral standards; what is morally right depends on culture/context |
| **Utilitarianism** (Mill) | Choose the act that does the greatest good for the greatest number (consequential) |
| **Deontology** (Kant) | An action is right because the *principle* guiding it is right, not because of consequences (nonconsequential) |
| **Egoism** | Actions taken out of self-interest |
| **Altruism** | Actions taken to benefit others |
| **Ethics of care / Relational ethics** (Gilligan) | Ethics exists in relationships; solution found in strengthening relationships, not abstract principles |
| **Virtue ethics** (Aristotle) | Positive character developed through consistently taking actions based on society's values; focus on the whole person |

### Other Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Fiduciary relationship** | Legal term: counselor acts in the client's benefit; client is not there for counselor's benefit |
| **Transference** | Client projects past feelings/attitudes toward significant people onto their therapist |
| **Countertransference** | Counselor's own feelings, attitudes, experiences that arise in work with a client |
| **Disclosure statement** | Written contract for informed consent; discloses nature of the counseling relationship |
| **Privileged communication** | Legal concept protecting clients from having confidential communications disclosed in court without permission |
| **Confidentiality** | Explicit contract/promise not to reveal client information except under agreed circumstances |
| **Privacy** | Broadest term; individual's right to decide which personal information is shared |
| **Malpractice** | Professional misconduct or unreasonable lack of skill resulting in harm to client |
| **Proximate cause** | Reasonably close causal connection between counselor's conduct and resulting injury |
| **Subpoena** | Legal document compelling production of records or testimony |
| **HIPAA** | Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act — federal law requiring specific written consent and privacy protections |
| **SOAP notes** | Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan — format for clinical notes |
| **DAP notes** | Data, Assessment, Plan — alternative note format |
| **Slippery slope** | When one begins to compromise principles, it becomes easier to continue sliding |

## 2. CHAPTER 1: INTRODUCTION TO ETHICS

### Professional Orientation Has 3 Components
1. **Developing a professional identity** as a counselor
2. **Learning about ethics** — standards, reasoning, decision-making skills
3. **Learning about the law** as it applies to counseling

### Model for Professional Practice (Figure 1-1)
"Built from within but balanced from outside the self"

**Internal building blocks** (bottom to top):
1. Intentionality — wanting to do the right thing
2. Moral principles & virtues
3. Knowledge of ethical, legal, and professional standards
4. Self-awareness
5. Decision-making skills and models
6. Courage of your convictions

**External supports:**
- Consultation with colleagues
- Supervision
- Continuing education
- Code of ethics
- Laws (e.g., privileged communication statutes)
- Agency/system policies

### Key Points from Chapter 1
- Ethics prevails over personal values when conflicts arise in counseling
- The counseling relationship exists to benefit the client
- Few absolute right answers exist — dilemmas require careful reasoning
- Ethical reasoning is an *acquired skill*, not an inherent gift
- ACA Code of Ethics standards must be interpreted considering uniqueness of client, situation, and cultural variables
- The "slippery slope" phenomenon: compromising principles becomes easier over time

## 3. CHAPTER 2: PROFESSIONAL IDENTITY OF COUNSELORS

### Consensus Definition of Counseling (20/20 Vision, 2010)
> "Counseling is a professional relationship that empowers diverse individuals, families, and groups to accomplish mental health, wellness, education, and career goals."
- Accepted by 29 of 31 major counseling organizations (School Counselors and Counselors for Social Justice did not accept at the time)

### Philosophy of Counseling: The Wellness Model
The counseling profession is distinct from psychology/psychiatry in its focus:
1. **Wellness model** (not illness/medical model) — assumes health
2. **Developmental perspective** — lifespan, stages, growth
3. **Prevention and early intervention** — preferred over remediation
4. **Individual empowerment** — teach clients to solve problems independently
- Terminal degree for licensure: **Master's degree** (unlike psychology which requires a doctorate)

### Other Mental Health Professions (p. 58-59)
| Profession | Degree | Focus |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| **Psychiatrist** | MD | Medical model, prescribing ability |
| **Clinical Psychologist** | PhD | Medical model, assessment, research, psychopathology |
| **Clinical Social Worker** | MA | Diversity, human rights, social/economic justice, policy |
| **Professional Counselor** | MA | Wellness, development, prevention, empowerment |

### CACREP (Council for Accreditation of Counseling and Related Educational Programs)
- Sets standards for counselor preparation
- Accredits training programs
- Helps with licensure portability
- Preps for NCE exam
- Foundation for doctoral studies
- 8 core areas all counseling students must study (including Professional Orientation and Ethical Practice, Social/Cultural Identities, Lifespan Development, etc.)

### Credentialing
- **Licensure** — most powerful; established by state law; required for practice/billing
- **Certification** — voluntary, shows competence/specialty (e.g., NCC from NBCC)
- **Accreditation** — program-level (CACREP)
- Licensure reciprocity across state lines is a current issue; Counseling Compact working toward this

### ACA (American Counseling Association)
- Primary professional organization: www.counseling.org
- 4 regions, 19 divisions, state branches
- Student rates available
- Professional values (ACA, 2014):
  1. Enhancing human development across the lifespan
  2. Honoring diversity
  3. Embracing a multicultural approach
  4. Promoting social justice
  5. Safeguarding the integrity of the counselor-client relationship
  6. Practicing competently and ethically

### Current Issues in Counseling
- Danger of fragmenting into specialties vs. one unified profession
- Standardization of licensure requirements across states
- Scope of practice disputes with other mental health professions
- Job classifications that don't include counseling titles
- Third-party reimbursement
- Telehealth standards
- Licensure reciprocity (Counseling Compact: counselingcompact.org)

## 4. CHAPTER 3: MULTICULTURALISM, SOCIAL JUSTICE, ADVOCACY & VALUES

### Key Concepts
- **ACA Code requires** counselors take an active role understanding clients' diverse cultural backgrounds
- **Multicultural counseling competency** is required across ALL counseling specialties (C.2.a.)
- Counselors wield power over clients; avoiding abuse of that power in cultural differences is an ethical responsibility

### Cultural Competency Components
1. **Attitudes and Beliefs** — awareness of social identities, biases, privileges
2. **Knowledge** — decrease assumptions, increase understanding of worldviews
3. **Skills** — reflective abilities, critical thinking, broaching, curiosity
4. **Actions** — advocating for change at individual and community levels

### ADDRESSING Model (Hays, 2002)
- **A**ge
- **D**evelopmental and acquired **D**isability
- **R**eligion/spirituality
- **E**thnicity
- **S**ocioeconomic status
- **S**exual orientation
- **I**ndigenous status
- **N**ationality
- **G**ender

### RESPECTFUL Model (D'Andrea & Daniels, 2001)
More psychological/individual components: Religious/spiritual ID, Ethnic ID, Sexual ID, Psychological ID, Economic class, Chronological challenges, Threats to wellbeing, Family history, Unique physical challenges, Location of residence

### Intersectionality (Crenshaw, 1989)
Compounded oppression for those with multiple minority identities

### Social Justice vs. Advocacy
- **Social justice** — promoting access for all people to mental health services, especially marginalized
- **Advocacy** — commitment to social justice through *action* to promote change

### Cultural Humility
- Recognizing our limitations
- Willingness to: seek consultation, seek continuing education, make referrals
- The most important resource for learning about cultural differences is **YOUR CLIENT**

### Values and Referrals
- **Appropriate referral reasons** (A.11.a, c): client no longer needs services, unlikely to benefit, at risk of harm, doesn't pay fees, counselor lacks competency, counselor in jeopardy
- **INAPPROPRIATE referral reasons** (A.11.b, d): discrimination, differences in values/attitudes/beliefs/personality, abandonment situations
- **A.11.b**: "Counselors refrain from referring prospective and current clients based solely on the counselor's personally held values"
- Professional counselors may NOT deny services to LGBTQ+ individuals (or any protected class) based on counselor's values
- Referrals must be based on **skill-based competency**, not values

### Bracketing in Practice
- Must know your own values to bracket them
- Without bracketing: emotional suppression, denial, or fantasy may occur (Sells & Hagadorn, 2016)
- James Marcia's Theory: identities should be defined internally, not externally (foreclosure)

## 5. CHAPTER 4: CLIENT WELFARE & INFORMED CONSENT

### Client Welfare
- Counselors are responsible for safeguarding clients' welfare from initiation through termination
- Counselors have a **fiduciary relationship** with clients (legal term: you act in their benefit)
- Must be aware of personal needs/motivations and countertransference reactions

### Countertransference Warning Signs
- Being overprotective, treating clients in overly kind ways
- Rejecting a client
- Needing constant reinforcement/approval
- Seeing yourself in clients
- Developing sexual/romantic feelings
- Giving advice compulsively
- Desiring social relationships with clients

### Mandated Clients (ACA A.2.e)
- May refuse services; counselor must explain consequences of refusal
- Must be accurate, honest, objective in evaluations (C.6.b)
- Court mandates often require disclosure to court
- Make clear if purpose is evaluation vs. counseling
- View counseling and evaluation as **two separate processes** (ideally two different clinicians)
- Written informed consent required to move from therapeutic to evaluative role

### Treatment Plans & Techniques
- Goals and treatment plans established **jointly** with clients
- Techniques grounded in **empirical research** (C.7.a)
- Experimental techniques require discussion and possibly written disclosure
- Potential risks and benefits must be disclosed

### Termination Reasons
- Client reached goals
- Client not benefiting or being harmed
- Client doesn't pay fees
- Counselor in jeopardy of harm
- Counselor or client moves
- Have a plan for unexpected termination

### Avoiding Abandonment (ACA A.12)
- Do NOT terminate when client is in crisis or angry
- Honestly discuss intention to terminate with specific reason
- Give client opportunity to make changes
- Give written notice
- Offer 1-3 termination sessions
- Give up to 3 good referrals
- Give emergency contact info
- Document in client file
- Give plenty of time to find new provider
- Transfer records promptly

### Informed Consent
- Both a **legal** and **ethical** obligation
- Demonstrates respect for autonomy and self-determination
- Integral to forming effective therapeutic alliance
- Written contracts = **disclosure statements**
- Ongoing process, both oral and written
- HIPAA and state laws require specific written consent prior to services

### 10 Elements of Informed Consent
1. Purposes, goals, techniques, procedures, limitations, potential risks and benefits
2. Counselor's qualifications
3. Information about termination and referrals
4. Emergency protocols and crisis resources
5. Role of technology (and backup plan if tech fails)
6. Implications of diagnosis and intended use of testing/reports
7. Fees and billing information
8. Confidentiality and its limitations
9. Right to obtain records and actively participate in counseling plans (see HIPAA requirements)
10. Right to refuse recommended services or modality change and be advised of consequences

### 8 Areas That Could Lead to Legal Problems (p. 100)
1. Failing to include required HIPAA elements
2. Guaranteeing an outcome
3. Guaranteeing privacy with no exceptions
4. Agreeing to unintended fee arrangements
5. Touching without permission
6. Misrepresenting credentials
7. Failing to communicate the nature of counseling
8. Neglecting to warn about possible stigma (of diagnosis)

### Diversity Considerations
- Informed consent as traditionally conceptualized may not be respectful of all cultures
- Use language clients can understand
- Consider cultural implications and adjust practices accordingly

## 6. CHAPTER 5: CONFIDENTIALITY & PRIVILEGED COMMUNICATION

### Three Related but Distinct Concepts
| Concept | Type | Definition |
|---------|------|-----------|
| **Privacy** | Broadest | Client's right to decide which information is shared |
| **Confidentiality** | Ethical | Explicit promise not to reveal client info except under agreed circumstances |
| **Privileged communication** | Legal | Protects clients from having communications disclosed in court without permission |

### Confidentiality
- "One of the most fundamental of all professional obligations in counseling"
- Cornerstone on which trust is built
- Based on principle of **fidelity** (keeping promises)
- Entire **Section B** of ACA Code is devoted to it (the only issue with its own section)
- Confidentiality cannot be guaranteed when more than one client is in the room (groups, family therapy)

### Limits to Confidentiality (Exceptions)
1. **Client signs a Release of Information (ROI)**
2. **Duty to warn** — client poses danger to identifiable others (including life-threatening diseases, B.2.c)
3. **Duty to protect** — client is danger to self (suicidal) or others
4. **Duty to report** — suspected abuse/neglect of children or vulnerable adults
5. **Involuntary hospitalization** proceedings
6. **Client brings lawsuit** against counselor
7. **Subpoena** from secretary of health in response to complaint
8. **Court-ordered disclosure** (B.2.d — counselor should still seek client consent or limit disclosure)

### Privileged Communication
- **Legal concept** (not ethical)
- Privilege belongs to the **CLIENT**, not the counselor
- Client must assert the privilege
- If client cannot be located, counselor asserts privilege on their behalf
- If client is deceased, legal representative asserts privilege

### WA State Relationships That Enjoy Privilege (RCW 5.60.060)
- Doctor/Patient
- Lawyer/Client
- Priest/Penitent
- Spouse/Spouse
- LMHC, LMFT, LCSW/Client
- Sexual Assault or DV Advocate
- AA Sponsor
- Psychologist/Patient
- **During practicum/internship, counseling students have the same ethical obligations to uphold confidentiality as licensed counselors**

### 9 Exceptions to Privileged Communication (Glosoff et al., 2000)
1. Complaints filed after counselor-client dispute
2. Client litigating their mental condition
3. Client danger to self or others
4. Report of child abuse
5. Client contemplating serious crime
6. Court-ordered psychological examination
7. Involuntary hospitalization proceedings
8. Client was victim of a crime
9. Client accused of harming a vulnerable adult

### Sharing Information
- Administrative staff handling confidential info: privilege extends to them
- Supervision: include in informed consent that you're getting supervision (ACA F.1.c); client identity usually shared
- Consultation: make every effort to protect client identity; get consent if cannot
- Referrals/past records: get client consent

### When More Than One Person Is Present
- Confidentiality cannot be guaranteed
- Family system or couple becomes identified client (unless otherwise established in informed consent)
- Discuss limits in informed consent before counseling begins

### Children Ages 13-18 in WA State
- **RCW 71.34.530**: Any minor 13+ may request and receive outpatient treatment without parental consent
- **RCW 70.02.130**: If minor is authorized to consent, only the minor may exercise patient rights regarding that care

## 7. CHAPTER 6: RECORDS & SUBPOENAS

### ACA on Records (B.6.a-h)
| Standard | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| B.6.a | Maintain records necessary for professional services |
| B.6.b | Records must be private/secure |
| B.6.c | Must have client permission to record sessions |
| B.6.d | Must have permission to observe/review sessions |
| B.6.e | Competent clients should have reasonable access to records |
| B.6.f | Provide assistance interpreting records |
| B.6.g | Written permission to disclose/transfer to third parties |
| B.6.h | Securely store records after termination; **5 years in WA** (WAC 246-809-035); be cautious about destroying records that may be needed in court |

### Why Keep Good Notes?
- Track goals, progress, change, turning points
- Document important actions/decisions (protects counselors if challenged)
- Remember where sessions left off
- Continuity of care if client changes counselors
- Write with understanding someone may read them later
- Clear enough for another professional to follow

### SOAP Notes
- **S**ubjective — client reported (perceptions, feelings, experiences)
- **O**bjective — counselor observed (hand-wringing, lack of eye contact, appearance)
- **A**ssessment — making sense of S and O
- **P**lan — action steps toward treatment goals

### DAP Notes
- **D**ata
- **A**ssessment
- **P**lan

### HIPAA Requirements for Counselors
- Give clients clear written explanation of how you use, keep, and disclose their health care information
- Give clients access to their records (unless it would cause them harm)
- Follow a written process for clients to request amendments
- Keep written history of disclosures, available to clients
- HIPAA exception: may share with family/others assisting in care if client informed and given opportunity to object

### If Client Requests Records
1. Document and get signed release
2. Explain risks of disclosure
3. Ensure records would not harm client
4. Ensure other clients' info not disclosed
5. Provide assistance reviewing records
6. Have clear written policy

### Subpoenas
- Used to: obtain records, compel testimony, require written responses, require appearance with records
- **If subpoenaed**: Don't ignore but get advice before responding; contact client; talk to supervisor/attorney; do NOT just hand over records; check if information is privileged; get ROI from client (B.2.d)

## 8. CHAPTER 7: COMPETENCE, ASSESSMENT & DIAGNOSIS

### What Is Counselor Competency?
- Complex construct on a continuum
- Ethical: "do no harm" (nonmaleficence is most salient)
- Skill: "perform at an acceptable level"
- Practice: "within the boundaries of competence"
- Offer ethical referrals when outside scope (not for discriminatory reasons)

### How Competence Is Established
1. **Graduate programs** — admissions, curriculum, supervised experience, gatekeeping, remediation
2. **Licensure** — most powerful credentialing; established by state law
3. **Certification** — voluntary evidence of competency/specialty
4. **Continuing education** — required by most licensure boards (ACA C.2.a)

### WA State Licensure
- **LMHC-A**: education requirements + application
- **LMHC**: education + application + passing NCE or NCMHCE + 3000 hours (1200 direct, 100 supervision) or 3 years full-time

### Maintaining Competence (4 methods)
1. **Continuing education** — required by most boards
2. **Peer review/consultation** — lifelong process; enhanced self-awareness, support, feedback
3. **Technology** — internet resources for staying current
4. **Making referrals** — when client exceeds your competence (based on competence, NOT discrimination)

### Erosion of Competence: Burnout & Impairment
- **Burnout**: decline in self-care, strained relationships, damaged emotional health
- **Impairment**: unable to perform responsibilities, putting clients at risk
  - Often associated with substance abuse and blurring of boundaries
  - Risk factors: environmental factors, vicarious traumatization, preexisting conditions
- **ACA C.2.g and F.5.b**: counselors must monitor themselves; if impaired, must limit/suspend/terminate professional responsibilities and seek assistance

### Assessment & Diagnosis
- Counselors have education/skills for evaluations
- Clients being assessed need to understand: process differs from counseling, what it entails, what the product will be, how it will be used
- Written informed consent required before forensic evaluations
- Expert witness: avoid unless agreed to; if required by job, be prepared to testify

### Testing
- Competence depends on training/experience with the specific test
- Clients have right to informed choice about participation
- Clients can expect explanation of results
- **Diversity issues**: consider validity, reliability, norming populations; place results in proper perspective with cultural factors

### Diagnosis
- Usually using DSM-5-TR (medical model)
- Often required for insurance reimbursement
- Clients must understand implications of any diagnosis
- Diversity considerations: culture affects how problems are defined and experienced (E.5.b); be aware of historical prejudices in misdiagnosis (E.5.c)

## 9. CHAPTER 8: MALPRACTICE & RESOLVING LEGAL/ETHICAL CHALLENGES

### Malpractice Definition
Professional misconduct or unreasonable lack of skill; failure to render services to the expected level, resulting in harm.

### 4 Elements Client Must Prove for Malpractice
1. Counselor had a **duty** to use reasonable care
2. Counselor **failed** to conform to required duty of care (breach)
3. Client was **injured**
4. **Proximate cause** — reasonably close causal connection between counselor's conduct and injury
5. Client suffered **actual loss** or damage

### Most Common Reasons for Malpractice Lawsuits
1. **Sexual relationships with clients** (biggest payouts)
   - WA state law (WAC 246-16-100): counselor may NEVER engage in sexual relationship with client OR former client — more restrictive than ACA (which has 5-year rule)
2. **Client suicide** (second leading cause)

### Clients at Risk for Suicide
- Confidentiality waived when disclosure necessary to protect from serious/foreseeable harm (B.2.a)
- First responsibility: determine if client may be in danger
- Legal duty to accurately evaluate suicide potential
- Can be held liable for overreacting AND underreacting
- Non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI) is separate from suicidal ideation — assess separately

### Suicide Assessment Questions
- Do you have a plan?
- Can you tell me specifically what you plan to do?
- Do you have access to the means?
- Have you ever attempted before? What happened?

### Guidelines for Assessing Suicidal Behavior
- Take direct verbal warnings seriously
- Pay attention to previous attempts
- Identify depression
- Be alert for hopelessness/helplessness
- Monitor severe anxiety/panic attacks
- Determine if they have a plan
- History of substance abuse
- Behavioral changes (giving away possessions, finalizing affairs)
- History of psychiatric treatment

### Clients at Risk for Harming Others
- ACA B.2.a: confidentiality does not apply when disclosure required to protect from serious/foreseeable harm
- Range of actions: least intrusive (promise not to harm) to most intrusive (involuntary commitment)
- Options: notify family, persuade voluntary commitment, notify police, call periodically

### If Formal Ethics Complaint Filed Against You
- Respond fully and in writing to all charges
- Consult with an attorney
- Submit documentation
- Contact professional liability insurance carrier
- **Do NOT contact the client** who filed the complaint

### If Angry Client Threatens to Sue
- Respond directly if possible
- Calm the person; listen to concerns
- Do not admit to wrongdoing
- Don't say anything you wouldn't want repeated under oath

### Guidelines for Preventing Problems
- Document everything
- Maintain good records
- Consult regularly
- Stay current with ethical standards
- Carry professional liability insurance
- Follow informed consent procedures

## 10. KEY LEGAL CASES

### Tarasoff v. Regents of University of California (1976)
- **Established**: Duty to warn and duty to protect
- **Facts**: Poddar told a doctor of his obsession and desire to kill Tatiana Tarasoff. Police were called; Poddar said he wouldn't act. He then killed Tarasoff.
- **Ruling**: Therapists must take reasonable steps to protect intended victims. May include warning victim, notifying police, or other necessary steps.
- **Key principle**: "Therapist must take reasonable steps to care and protect the intended victim"

### Volk v. DeMeerleer (WA State)
- **Expanded Tarasoff** in Washington State
- **Facts**: DeMeerleer (history of severe mental illness, rageful thoughts) murdered his girlfriend Rebecca. Psychiatrist Ashby reported SI but no plan to act, assessed stability. DeMeerleer never identified a specific victim.
- **Ruling**: "A therapist's duty is NOT limited to a duty to warn in WA. The duty to protect can arise even if the client does NOT express an intention to harm a reasonably identifiable victim."
- **Key principle**: Broader duty to protect in WA than Tarasoff requires

### Other Important Cases (from Lecture 3 slides)
| Case | Established |
|------|-------------|
| **Bradley Case** | Duty not to negligently release a dangerous client |
| **Jablonski Case** | Duty to commit a dangerous individual |
| **Hedlund Case** | Extends duty to warn to anyone near the intended victim who might also be in danger |
| **Jaffee Case** | Communications between licensed psychotherapists and clients are privileged and protected from forced disclosure in federal cases |

## 11. ACA CODE OF ETHICS QUICK REFERENCE

### Structure
- Preamble + 8 Sections (A-I):
  - **A**: The Counseling Relationship
  - **B**: Confidentiality and Privacy
  - **C**: Professional Responsibility
  - **D**: Relationships with Other Professionals
  - **E**: Evaluation, Assessment, and Interpretation
  - **F**: Supervision, Training, and Teaching
  - **G**: Research and Publication
  - **H**: Distance Counseling, Technology, and Social Media
  - **I**: Resolving Ethical Issues

### 6 Purposes of the ACA Code
1. Set forth ethical obligations and guidance
2. Identify ethical considerations for counselors and trainees
3. Clarify ethical responsibilities held in common
4. Serve as guide for constructing best course of action
5. Support the mission of ACA
6. Serve as basis for processing ethics complaints

### Most Testable ACA Standards by Section

#### Section A: The Counseling Relationship
| Standard | Key Content |
|----------|-------------|
| **A.1.a** | Primary responsibility: respect dignity and promote welfare of clients |
| **A.1.c** | Counseling plans devised jointly; regularly reviewed |
| **A.1.d** | Consider support networks with client consent |
| **A.2.a** | Informed consent: ongoing, documented, both written and verbal |
| **A.2.b** | Types of information needed (purposes, goals, techniques, fees, confidentiality limits, etc.) |
| **A.2.c** | Developmental and cultural sensitivity in communication |
| **A.2.d** | Minors/incapacitated adults: seek assent; balance rights |
| **A.2.e** | Mandated clients: discuss limitations, explain info sharing, client may refuse |
| **A.4.a** | Avoiding harm |
| **A.4.b** | Personal values: avoid imposing; respect diversity; seek training |

## 12. 8-STEP ETHICAL DECISION-MAKING MODEL

*(From text p. 19-21 and Lectures 1 & 2)*

Keep in mind: **cultural context** and **your emotions** throughout.

1. **Identify the problem or dilemma** — Is it a legal issue or a clinical issue? Take time to review carefully.
2. **Involve your client** in the decision-making process — empowers the client, builds the relationship, safeguards integrity.
3. **Review relevant ethics codes** — review codes, laws, and literature.
4. **Consider the principles and virtues** — not just what to DO but who you want to BE.
5. **Tune into your feelings** — aware of emotions, beliefs, values, motivations. Body relaxed, mind engaged.
6. **CONSULT with colleagues or experts** — important from a legal standpoint too.
7. **Consider the context** — be culturally competent; consider ramifications for client's family, community, other professionals.
8. **Make a decision and act** — don't linger in indecision; have courage.

## 13. WASHINGTON STATE SPECIFIC LAWS

### Licensure
- **LMHC-A**: education requirements + application
- **LMHC**: education + NCE/NCMHCE + 3000 hours (1200 direct, 100 supervision) or 3 years full-time
- 50 hours postgraduate supervision for CACREP grads

### Privileged Communication (RCW 5.60.060)
- Extends to: Doctor/Patient, Lawyer/Client, Priest/Penitent, Spouse/Spouse, LMHC/LMFT/LCSW/Client, DV/SA Advocates, AA Sponsors, Psychologist/Patient

### Sexual Relationships with Clients (WAC 246-16-100)
- **NEVER** — with current OR former clients
- **More restrictive** than ACA (which allows after 5 years with documentation)

### Records Retention (WAC 246-809-035)
- **5 years** after termination in WA

### Minors and Consent
- **Outpatient mental health**: age 13+ can consent without parental consent (RCW 71.34.530)
- **Outpatient substance abuse**: age 13+
- **STD testing/treatment**: age 14+
- **Birth control**: any age
- **Sexual activity age of consent**: 16 (Chapter 9A.44 RCW)
  - 14-15: partner must be less than 4 years older
  - 12-13: partner must be less than 3 years older
  - Under 12: partner must be less than 2 years older

### Family-Initiated Treatment (for kids 13+)
- **Outpatient**: parent may seek services for up to 12 sessions in 3 months without adolescent consent; then adolescent must consent (RCW 71.34.650)
- **Inpatient**: adolescent may give written notice to leave at any time

### Child Abuse & Neglect (RCW 26.44)
- **Definition** (RCW 26-44-020): injury, sexual abuse, sexual exploitation, negligent treatment, maltreatment indicating child's health/welfare/safety is harmed
- Physical discipline is NOT abuse when reasonable and moderate by parent/teacher/guardian (RCW 9A.16.100)
- **Presumed unreasonable**: throwing, kicking, burning, cutting; striking with closed fist; shaking child under 3; interfering with breathing; threatening with deadly weapon; causing bodily harm greater than transient pain
- **Mandatory reporters** must report (RCW 26.44)
- Report to: 1-866-END HARM (1-866-363-4276) or county numbers
- King County: 1-800-609-8764

### Vulnerable Adults (WA Definition)
- Person 60+ lacking functional/physical/mental ability to care for self
- Adult with developmental disability
- Adult with legal guardian
- Adult in long-term care facility
- Adult receiving home services

### Death with Dignity Act (RCW 70.245)
- Passed November 4, 2008
- Clients with less than 6 months to live may request lethal medication
- ACA allows counselors option of confidentiality
- Seek supervision and legal advice

### Ricky's Law
- Procedures for reporting individuals at risk of suicide/self-harm
- Be familiar with your site's procedures

### Duty to Warn/Protect in WA
- **Broader than Tarasoff** per *Volk v. DeMeerleer*
- Duty to protect can arise even without identified victim or expressed homicidal ideation
- Therapist must act with "reasonable care, consistent with the standards of the mental health profession"

## 14. CHRISTIAN INTEGRATION (WATSON, 2018)

### Character Virtues Mapped to Professional Ethics & Spiritual Disciplines

| Character Virtue | Professional Ethic | Spiritual Disciplines |
|-----------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **Faith** | Fidelity | Contemplative prayer & church involvement |
| **Hope** | Beneficence | Sabbath & worship |
| **Love** | Respect/Autonomy | Intercessory prayer |
| **Justice** | Justice | Attentiveness, hospitality & service |
| **Temperance** | Nonmaleficence | Examen & fasting |
| **Courage** | Integrity/Veracity | Rule of life & example |
| **Practical Wisdom** | Competence | Scripture & discernment |

### Faith and Fidelity (Watson, 2018)
- Aquinas: faith involves "believing someone and believing something"
- Watson argues faith connects to trust; therapists live out faith through building trust in relationships
- Fidelity = same root as fiduciary: "honoring commitments and keeping promises"
- Cultivating faith: contemplative prayer and church involvement
- "We uphold the ethical principle of fidelity not only because we have to but because it has become an essential part of our personal and professional character" (Watson, 2018, p. 46-47)

### Justice (Watson, 2018; Aquinas)
- Aquinas: "to render to each person that which is his or her right"
- Other-oriented virtue (vs. other cardinal virtues that are individually oriented)
- ACA focus: prosocial behavior, equal access, treating individuals equitably
- Christian doctrine: all persons created by God with eternal value
- Key scriptures: Micah 6:8 ("act justly, love mercy, walk humbly"), Isaiah 1:17, Psalm 106:3, Isaiah 61:1/Luke 4

### Wisdom and Discernment
- Dallas Willard: study as spiritual discipline — "focusing the mind upon God's works and words"
- Ignatius of Loyola: discernment as a habit — "finding God in all things"
- Ruth Haley Barton: prayerful discernment includes collecting data, inner reflection, action, submission to God's will

### Developing Virtue (Neuroscience Findings)
1. Developing ingrained habits of moral thought/action over time (automatic cognitive processes)
2. Moral exemplars facilitate moral thinking (mirror neuron research)
3. Religious communities are effective contexts for modeling virtuous habits

### Exclusion and Embrace (Volf, 2019)
- "Double vision" — seeing through another's perspective empathically without leaving behind own views
- High level of differentiation between self and others required
- Paul in Romans 3:23: "all have fallen short" — understanding our part in cultural sin
