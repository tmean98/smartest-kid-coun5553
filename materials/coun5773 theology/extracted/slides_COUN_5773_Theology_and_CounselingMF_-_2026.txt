Course Description:  
 This course presents a Christian theological perspective and encourages the student to think deeply about their own beliefs about the nature of humans in light of contemporary issues in the field of counseling. 
 
Course Objectives:  
The student will: 
Compose a self-narrative that identifies religious, spiritual, and ethical self-understanding in relation to the vocation of mental health counseling (CACREP Standard 2b, 2d, and 2g). 
Assess anthropological assumptions in religious and philosophical texts (CACREP Standard 2a, 2b, 2d, 2g, and 2h). 
Summarize several written communications on the integration of faith and mental health counseling for use by peers (CACREP Standard 2d and 2g). 
Construct an integrative research paper that develops faith and philosophical sources in an integration with counseling ethics, justice, and advocacy (CACREP Standard 2b, 2d, 2g, and 2g). 
 
CACREP Standards:  
 2F.2.a. multicultural and pluralistic characteristics within and among diverse groups nationally and internationally 
 2F.2.b. theories and models of multicultural counseling, cultural identity development, and social justice and advocacy 
 2F.2.d. the impact of heritage, attitudes, beliefs, understandings, and acculturative experiences on an individual's views of others 
 2F.2.g. the impact of spiritual beliefs on clients' and counselors' worldviews  2F.2.h. strategies for identifying and eliminating barriers, prejudices, and processes of intentional and unintentional oppression and discrimination

Academic Honesty: 
Northwest University expects honesty from students in all areas, including their academic lives. Academic dishonesty is a serious violation of University standards and may result in the students' receiving an "F" on the assignment, receiving an "F" for the course, or possibly being dismissed from the University. Instances of academic dishonesty are coordinated through the respective graduate program office and typically reported to the Provost's Office. 
Academic dishonesty includes cheating on assignments or examinations, submitting the same (or essentially the same) paper in more than one course without prior consent of the current assigning professor(s), sabotaging another student's work, and plagiarizing. Plagiarism is "using someone else's ideas or phrasing and representing those ideas or phrasing as our own, either on purpose or through carelessness" (MLA).  Any student disciplined by a faculty member for alleged academic dishonesty has the right to appeal the disciplinary action. The student should initiate his/her appeal according to procedures outlined in the Northwest University Student Academic Appeals Policy, which is available in the Provost Office.  
 
Attendance Policy:   
Class attendance is extremely important for student learning and achievement of specified course outcomes and, in turn, the program aims. Hence, attendance at all class sessions is expected. At minimum, students absent for a class session will need to make up any missed assignments or exercises and complete the expected readings.  A grade penalty will be applied if more than one class session is missed. Additional policies and consequences are at the discretion of the individual professor of the course. More than one missed class session will result in failure of the course and require a retake to continue in the program. In this circumstance, the student is encouraged to withdraw from the course and retake the class when it is offered again. In the event of unusual circumstances (e.g., extended illness, emergency), a student may appeal the automatic course failure. A proposal outlining a plan for satisfactory course completion will be required, composed by the professor and agreed to by the student. In addition, the plan will require approval by the program director and Dean. 
 
Disability Accommodations and Procedures:

Discussion Boards (20%)   
  
The weekly discussion boards, guided by a question prompt, require a 250-plus word evaluative response from the student. Students will be graded according to their response and will not be required to respond to others. Feedback should be constructive and on topic only, even and especially when challenging the ideas of others. However, the main purpose of the discussion board is for students to provide an evaluative response to a topic to develop evaluations of topics in our subject matter and development in quality of written communication. (Due on the Sunday following class by midnight) 
 
Vocational-Spiritual Self-Narrative (10% of course grade):   
 
Who am I? Who am I in the present, and who have I been? Who am I in my social relations? Who am I in my vocational calling? Who am I in relationship to God (or my conception of ultimate reality)? Who am I as a person studying mental health counseling as a next step in the journey of my calling in life? 
  
Written communication: A 1000-1500 word narrative essay that a) is carefully composed with no or almost no writing errors; b) provides response to the above questions, though the structure of how may vary; c) demonstrates self-understanding and respect for others; d) responds to some of the spiritual, theological, and philosophical notions found in Smith and other assigned course readings, along with other valuable sources at the writer's discretion; and e) APA-formatted citations as necessary.  (Due on February 22)

Faith and Counseling Integration Paper (40%)  
  
A 2000-3000-word paper which includes at least three major sections, developing the following areas:   
  
Construct a researched description of several theological and/or philosophical affirmations you hold regarding the human person or human communities.  
  
Develop a moral basis for an ethical approach to mental health counseling in relation to your researched description above. 
  
Describe an area of advocacy in which you envision practically applying the above ethical approach to a concrete area of justice or service.  
  
The paper should include six (6) or more scholarly sources identified through the student's own research. (Due April 19) 
 
Field Work: Anthropological Observation (10%) 
 
A 750-1000-word observational paper with attendant documentation observing embedded anthropologies in a socio-cultural setting or a person or persons in their relational setting.  Choose one. (Due on April 5) 
Options A or B will be based on "A Phenomenology of Cultural Liturgies" by J.K.A. Smith. 
Option A: A Christian or another worship observation on the human self before God and with others in religious communities  
Option B: A common or secular practice, anthropological observation on understandings of the human self.
Options C or D will be based on the Integration tools from class discussions.
Option C: Relational EKG - Interview 2 people to assess their positive and negative relations to persons, places, and activities.
Option D: Relational X-Ray – Interview two people on their relational contexts, movements, and progress toward healthy relating.
