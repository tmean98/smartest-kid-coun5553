Instructions for Field Work: Anthropological Observations (10% each/x2)
Field experience takes place through observations of the anthropologies embedded in one of two
settings: 1) in a setting of Christian worship or another site of religious practice and 2) another
event or setting which identifies an anthropology embedded in a common socio-cultural site or
practice. These should range from between 750-1000 words.
The assignment addresses the following (in an approximately 80% observation/interpretation
and 20% evaluative proportion):
 What is the underlying (implicit) anthropology (understanding of the human) in this
event?
 What is the human (according to the underlying assumptions)?
 What are expectations of the persons involved in this event and how do these
expectations reflect the norms and underlying ideas about humanity which this
community or social situation or event assumes?
 What does this event say about human purposes?
 What is going on that is habituating people, leading them into a way of life in this
event?
 And, briefly, how do you evaluate what you have observed and interpreted here?
The concept behind this assignment is that practices, habits, “liturgies” embed or teach or habituate
understandings of the human into human beings, so that they teach us what the human is through
our doing and being and becoming.
What does the event that you observed do to form humans?

Additional Prompts for Anthropological Observations
Who are the human beings in this event expected to be?
What are some of the roles that are being played? How might some of these roles relate to the
whole lives of the persons involved?
What does this event assume about the purposes for the human beings involved in it? What does
this event assume about the purposes of other persons?
What does the naming of people have to do with the understanding of the humans involved in this
event?
What do the human power structures involved in this event look like? What is assumed? What
might be contested?
What is the “unthought” going on about the humans involved in this event and humans in general?
That is, what is so deeply assumed here that it might be an epiphany to recognize as a powerful
assumption guiding this event?
How might this event relate to the larger life of this community in which the event is taking place?
What are the explicit beliefs that are espoused during this event? What are some beliefs that seem
to be implicit in it and the community?
What seems to give meaning to this event? How does that meaning resonate with the participants,
or not?
What are some of the ethical structures embedded in this event and, as you might infer from your
observation, perhaps the wider community? How might the personal moralities of the persons
involved in the event or in the community relate to these ethical structures?
Was there anything odd or surprising to you about the expectations of those involved in this event?
Did you find things to be moral or immoral, ethical or unethical?
Did you find things to be spiritually enriching or problematic or harmful or anything else along
these lines?
What kind of humans is this event shaping those involved in it to be?
