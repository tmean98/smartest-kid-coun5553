How to Use the DSM 5
This document is not intended to be an exhaustive set of instructions for using the DSM 5. Rather, it is a quick, basic guide on how to approach using the DSM 5 and its related tools. 
Using a diagnostic interview, determine the client’s primary area of complaint (e.g. depression, anxiety, sexual dysfunction, sleep issues, etc.) 
As a starting point in your intake process you also include the Crosscutting Tools provided by the APA. See the measure online at https://www.psychiatry.org/psychiatrists/practice/dsm/educational-resources/assessment-measures 
Read about the primary area of complaint. 
Start with the Diagnostic Features. Consider if the symptoms and experiences described in the DSM fit with those described by your client. 
If so, focus on the Diagnostic Criteria. Be sure you understand the specific requirements for a diagnosis of that disorder. Remember each disorder has different criteria and you must be sure a client meets the appropriate number of criteria for a diagnosis.
If the client meets criteria move on to differential diagnosis.
According to First (2014), the steps in the process of differential diagnosis are ruling out malingering (faking symptoms), rule out substance related symptoms/causes, rule out medical related causes, determine the primary disorder, explore adjustment disorder, and finally determine if the client meets full criteria for a mental disorder.
If not, move on the next possible condition and repeat.
Additional considerations to be aware of are cultural differences in presentation of symptoms, and understanding prevalence rates for disorder. This information is included for each disorder in the DSM.
