Clinical Interviewing- Assessment for Suicide
Assessment for risk of suicide can be challenging
Individual moods and experience shift and change between/after session
Clients’ experiences may be similar but are unique.
Therefore, assessment must be considered unique and evaluated based on client, not just common signs.

The Art of Asking Questions
Be direct and brief but gentle
Ask about what you want to know
Avoid vague questions that leave the client guessing
Avoid asking more than one questions at a time.
Start with open questions 
What is that like for you.
Please tell me more about your experience…
Guide toward specificity
Closed questions help with this
Type, severity, frequency, duration- When, where, how, …
Adapted from Morrison, 2014
The Art of Asking Questions
Use the client’s language and avoid jargon
Get to “Why” Without asking why questions
Avoid asking the in the negative – “you don’t experience thoughts of suicide, do you?”
Avoid Leading questions- explore but do not prime or lead the client to get the information you want
Explore emotions- Pay attention to feeling words and ask directly
Adapted from Morrison, 2014
Practice
